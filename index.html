Computers are getting smarter.

If you have opened a web browser at any point in the last three years, you have felt this shift. Three years ago, you could ask Siri to play the new Taylor Swift album and get something like, "Sorry, I cannot help you tailor a suit." Now you can ask ChatGPT to explain quantum physics, or explain why your life is falling apart, and it will usually do a pretty good job at both.
But there is one part of the computing experience that still feels weirdly frozen in time. We still write down grocery lists even though most of us are basically buying the same things every week. We still try to find that note we wrote a month ago about a restaurant our friend recommended, except we cannot remember what we named it. And we still put notes into folders like it is 1998 and Joe from legal is about to ask for an update on the Johnson case. Sorry. I have been watching a lot of Seinfeld recently.
I use these tools heavily, and for years I kept running into the same question: Why am I still doing this in such an antiquated way? My engineer brain keeps telling me that the computer should be helping more than this. We built systems that can rewrite the C compiler, and meanwhile I am still manually extracting to-do items from meeting notes like an animal.
So to resolve that tension, I built Stonefruit. Stonefruit is a notes app that gets smarter overnight, on your own hardware, using markdown and automation.

One of the first things you notice about Stonefruit is that it is built on markdown. If you are not familiar with markdown, it is just a lightweight way of typing that turns plain text into richer documents. I chose markdown because it is based on plaintext. That makes it portable. If you start using Stonefruit and later decide you do not like it, that is fine. There is the door. You can take all your notes with you. It also turns out that markdown is a very model-friendly format, which matters once we start talking about automation.

LLMs are good at generating text, but they are also good at representing meaning. That is what embeddings are for. The problem is that good embedding models can be slow on client devices, especially older phones. So if your server has enough memory, Stonefruit can crawl your notes overnight and index them with a stronger embedding model. That means you can search for something like "pasta recipe" and still surface the note that contains your carbonara recipe, even if the exact words do not match.
