A notes app that gets smarter overnight, on your hardware.

Markdown. Self-hosted. Smart automation.

So to resolve that tension, I built Stonefruit. Stonefruit is a notes app that gets smarter overnight, on your own hardware, using markdown and automation.

What makes Stonefruit different

Markdown

Your notes are plain text, portable, and easy for both humans and models to work with.

Self-hosted

Your server handles sync and automation. Your notes stay on hardware you control.

Overnight automations

The app can keep organizing, indexing, and improving your notes while you are asleep.

There are three ideas underneath Stonefruit. Markdown keeps the format durable and portable. Self-hosting keeps your notes private and under your control. And overnight automations are the part that actually makes the system feel intelligent.

Built on markdown

Simple to write. Rich when rendered. Plain text underneath.

What you type

# Grocery list

- eggs
- pasta
- parmesan

Call Sam about dinner on Friday.

What you get

Grocery list

  • eggs
  • pasta
  • parmesan

Call Sam about dinner on Friday.

One of the first things you notice about Stonefruit is that it is built on markdown. If you are not familiar with markdown, it is just a lightweight way of typing that turns plain text into richer documents. I chose markdown because it is based on plaintext. That makes it portable. If you start using Stonefruit and later decide you do not like it, that is fine. There is the door. You can take all your notes with you. It also turns out that markdown is a very model-friendly format, which matters once we start talking about automation.

Run it on your own hardware

Private by default

Your notes are often the most personal data you have. Stonefruit is designed so that data stays on machines you trust.

Home base

A small server can handle sync plus the heavier automations that would be slow on a phone or laptop.

visual: your Raspberry Pi or home notes server

Stonefruit is also self-hosted. My notes are precious to me, and they are private. I care who has access to them. So I set Stonefruit up to run on your own hardware. That machine becomes home base for sync and for some of the heavier automations that run overnight. No calls to third parties. Nobody else gets to see your notes.

Overnight automations

When you stop thinking about your notes, Stonefruit does not.

The goal is to give your notes something like background cognition.

This is the secret sauce. Neurobiology tells us that even when we are not consciously thinking about a problem, our brain is still processing it in the background. That is why good ideas sometimes show up in the shower. I wanted to build something a little like that into Stonefruit.

Semantic search

Simple search

Fast, literal, and limited. Great when you already know the exact word you used.

Embedding search

Search by meaning. Ask for "pasta recipe" and still find the note named "carbonara".

visual: embedding diagram + simple search vs semantic search results

LLMs are good at generating text, but they are also good at representing meaning. That is what embeddings are for. The problem is that good embedding models can be slow on client devices, especially older phones. So if your server has enough memory, Stonefruit can crawl your notes overnight and index them with a stronger embedding model. That means you can search for something like "pasta recipe" and still surface the note that contains your carbonara recipe, even if the exact words do not match.

Untitled no more

The problem

Files need names. Humans do not always want to stop and think of one.

The automation

Stonefruit can infer what a note is about, learn your naming style, and suggest a better title.

visual: folder full of Untitled notes turning into clear titles

We are also adding other automations. One of my favorites is called "Untitled no more." Because of how files work, every note needs a name. Some people enjoy naming things carefully. Some people think that is a waste of time. Fine. With this automation, Stonefruit can look at the note, understand your usual naming style, and suggest a title that is actually useful.
